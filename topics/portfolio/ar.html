Curator's Note

"eyes on art" is a visual commentary on attention - where we place it, and what that says about us in shared creative spaces. on the far left, there's a girl staring directly at the camera, and next to her, a man mid-conversation. both are holding tote bags that read "eyes on art". these bags aren't just accessories — they serve as subtle instructions. it's almost as if the bags are reminding them (and us) to stay present, to engage with the art around us rather than just exist alongside it. move toward the center of the image, and you'll notice a dj setup and a small group gathered around it. most of them are either making or observing art. but if you notice, one person stands apart - not because of what he's doing, but because of what he's not. he's on his phone. a black rectangle covers his eyes - a deliberate choice to symbolize his disconnect. his eyes are elsewhere, not on the art, not on the moment. the best part about "eyes on art" is that there was actually a piece in the exhibition that said "eyes on my art." i grabbed that concept and conveyed the message in my way.

Curator's Note

i happened to take these two pictures at the same time. they were placed right next to each other at the gallery, and even though they're clearly different images, i wanted the left one to feel like it's part of the right-like they belong together, but not entirely. maybe that's why i didn't try to fill up the whole frame; i wanted that tension. another thing i did intentionally was add color to just a part of the baby's face. i remember reading the curator's note-it said the beer baby represents purity and control, while the beer stands for recklessness. so the small area of color is my way of showing purity, and the rest left uncolored symbolizes recklessness.also, something i do with my photos is change the exposure or color tone of one of the images. it's not really random-it's how i like to see the world, and it's part of how i process it visually.
