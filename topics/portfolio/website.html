ANTI%ALGORITHM

Website Code Threejs

DESCRIPTION

Anti-Algorithm is a website project that explores how we might experience art outside the influence of social media algorithms. Today, most platforms curate what we see based on our previous behavior, which can make discovery feel repetitive and predictable. This project reflects on my own experiences of encountering music, clothing, and visual art without algorithmic recommendation, and attempts to guide users toward a similar form of exploration. The website consists of ten pages. The homepage features a spinning cassette model designed in Blender, with two conceptual sides: Side A represents my personal experience discovering art without algorithmic guidance, while Side B was initially intended to act as a guide for users. Although this dual structure did not fully function as originally imagined, it helped frame the overall concept of the site. The following pages build on this idea: Archive documents moments where I encountered art organically; Misfires highlights instances where exploration led me outside my usual tastes—sometimes resulting in things I disliked, but other times revealing unexpected favorites. This unpredictability is presented as the strength of discovery beyond algorithms, which often reinforce familiar patterns. The Maps section offers suggestions on how to search for music, clothing, and art independently, while Plant Room presents a visual environment that reflects the feeling of being trapped within algorithmic systems. The final section, Journal, allows users to type whatever they want; once they press enter, the text disappears, leaving no record or data trail. While algorithms are largely inevitable in contemporary digital culture, Anti-Algorithm serves as a small experimental space that encourages users to rediscover art through curiosity, chance, and personal exploration.

PROCESS

The project was developed as a multi-page interactive website combining 3D elements, visual design, and user interaction. The homepage cassette was modeled in Blender and used as a visual metaphor for analog media and the idea of “sides” or perspectives. From there, the site structure evolved into a series of thematic pages that each represent different aspects of discovery outside algorithmic systems. During the design process, I experimented with ways to translate personal experiences of wandering through art spaces such as record stores, clothing shops, or galleries into digital interactions. Pages like Archive and Misfires were created to reflect the randomness and unpredictability of exploration. Rather than presenting only successful discoveries, I wanted to include moments of failure or discomfort, since encountering things outside one’s taste is part of genuine exploration. The project also incorporates visual and conceptual experimentation. For example, Plant Room uses imagery and atmosphere to evoke a sense of confinement within algorithmic environments, while Journal removes permanence by deleting user input immediately after submission. This interaction intentionally contrasts with typical online platforms where every action is stored and analyzed. Although algorithms are an unavoidable part of how digital platforms function today, this project was created as a playful and reflective experiment. It imagines small ways users might step outside algorithmic systems and rediscover art through chance, curiosity, and personal movement rather than automated recommendation.
